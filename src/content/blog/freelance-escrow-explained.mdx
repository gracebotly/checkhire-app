## What Is Freelance Escrow and Why Should You Care?

**Freelance escrow** is a payment arrangement where a neutral third party holds a client's money until you, the freelancer, deliver the work you agreed to. That's it. The client can't ghost you after you finish the work, and you can't disappear with an upfront payment. Both sides are protected.

If you've ever done freelance work for someone you found on Reddit, Twitter, a Discord server, or a Facebook group, you already know the risk. You spend 10 hours building something, send the final files, and then... silence. The client vanishes. Your invoice goes unpaid. You have zero recourse.

Or flip it around: a client sends $500 to a freelancer via Venmo for a website redesign, the freelancer delivers garbage (or nothing at all), and that money is gone forever.

Freelance escrow solves both of these problems. The money exists. It's locked. Neither party can touch it until the job is done. This is how escrow works for freelancers, and it's the single most effective way to prevent getting scammed on gig work.

## How Freelance Escrow Works: Step by Step

The escrow process for freelancers is straightforward. There are four stages, and each one exists to prevent a specific type of scam.

### Step 1: The Client Funds the Escrow

Before any work begins, the client deposits the full payment amount into escrow. This money is held by the platform (in this case, CheckHire) in a secure account. The freelancer can see that the funds are locked and verified.

**What this prevents:** The classic "do the work and I'll pay you later" scam. If the escrow isn't funded, you don't start. Period.

### Step 2: The Freelancer Does the Work

With the money safely locked, you do your thing. Design the logo, write the copy, build the app, edit the video. Whatever the gig is. You work according to the agreed-upon scope and deadline.

**What this prevents:** Nothing, really. This is just the work phase. But you work with confidence because you know the money is already set aside.

### Step 3: The Freelancer Delivers

You submit your deliverables through the platform. The client gets notified and has a window to review the work. On CheckHire, the client has 72 hours to either approve the delivery or open a dispute.

**What this prevents:** Clients who receive perfect work and then claim they never got it. The delivery is timestamped, logged, and visible to both parties.

### Step 4: Funds Are Released

Once the client approves, the money releases to your account immediately. If the client doesn't respond within 72 hours, the funds auto-release to you. No chasing invoices. No awkward follow-up emails.

**What this prevents:** The slow-ghost — clients who "forget" to pay, go on vacation, or simply stop responding after receiving their deliverables.

The "trust me bro" column exists because it's still the most common payment method for freelancers working outside of major platforms. Someone in a Discord DM says "I'll PayPal you when it's done." That's not a payment system. That's a hope-based economy.

## When Should You Use Escrow? A Simple Decision Framework

Not every transaction needs escrow. Here's a straightforward framework for deciding when escrow payment for gig work makes sense:

**Always use escrow when:**
- The gig is worth more than $50
- You found the client outside a trusted platform (Reddit, Discord, Twitter, Facebook groups, Telegram)
- You've never worked with this client before
- There's no written contract (and even if there is, contracts are hard to enforce for small amounts)
- The client is in a different country
- The project will take more than a few hours

**You can probably skip escrow when:**
- You're working with a repeat client who has paid you reliably multiple times
- The gig is under $50 and you can afford to lose it
- You're working through a platform that already has built-in payment protection (though most charge you 10-20% for it)
- The client is a registered business with a verifiable track record and you have a signed contract

The threshold is really about risk tolerance. If losing the payment would hurt — financially or emotionally — use escrow. The cost to the client is minimal, and any serious client will understand why you're asking for it. If a client refuses to use escrow, that tells you everything you need to know about whether they intend to pay.
